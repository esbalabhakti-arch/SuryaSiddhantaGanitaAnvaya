गुरुर् देवो महेश्वरः ।
गुरुः साक्षात् परं ब्रह्म
तस्मै श्री गुरवे नमः ॥

गुरवे सर्वलोकानां
भिषजे भव रोगिणाम् ।
निधये सर्वविद्यनाम्
दक्षिणामूर्तये नमः ॥

अस्मिन् गुरु चरणारविन्दाभ्यां नमः ॥

Today, April 17th, as I continue this journey into the Sūryा Siddhānta, I remind myself that I am first a student — trying to slowly understand what this text is revealing. In the previous session, we saw the beginning of a very powerful moment — Maya performing intense tapas and being graced by the appearance of Bhagavān Sūrya. That itself was striking, because this is not just a scientific text suddenly starting with definitions or equations — it begins with tapas, with humility, with seeking. And then Sūrya appears, ready to teach Jyotiṣa. But right there, a subtle question begins to form in my mind — how can Sūrya, whose very nature is blazing brilliance, actually communicate this knowledge? Can such an intense presence really teach in a way that can be received? And even deeper than that — is this knowledge something new being revealed to Maya for the first time, or is it something ancient, something that has always existed and is simply being passed on again? These are the thoughts that naturally arise from the earlier verses, and today, as I move into verses 6 to 10, I expect the text itself to start answering these questions in its own precise and elegant way.

Okay… now let us recite verses 6 to 10 first, and then let us do a detailed review of each line.

Verse-by-Verse Meaning
🔹 Verse 6
न मे तेज: सह: किश्चदाख्यातुं नास्ति मे क्षण: ।
मदंश: पुरुषोऽयं ते निश्शेषं कथियष्यति ॥

Word-by-word meaning:
न → not
मे → my
तेज: → brilliance
सह: → tolerable / bearable
किञ्चित् → anything
आख्यातुम् → to explain / to tell
नास्ति → is not
मे → for me
क्षण: → even a moment
मदंश: → a portion of myself
पुरुष: अयम् → this person
ते → to you
निश्शेषम् → completely
कथियष्यति → will explain

Overall meaning:
“My brilliance is not bearable even for a moment to explain anything directly; therefore, this person, who is a part of myself, will explain everything to you completely.”

🔹 Verse 7
इत्युक्त्वाऽन्तर्दधे देव: समादिश्यांशमात्मन: ।
स पुमान् मयमाहेदं प्रणतं प्राञ्जलिस्थितम् ॥

Word-by-word meaning:
इति उक्त्वा → having said thus
अन्तर्दधे → disappeared
देव: → the divine (Sun)
समादिश्य → having instructed
अंशम् आत्मन: → a part of himself
स: पुमान् → that person
मयम् → to Maya
आह → said
इदम् → this
प्रणतम् → who had bowed
प्राञ्जलि स्थितम् → standing with folded hands

Overall meaning:
“Having said this, the divine Sun disappeared after appointing a part of himself; that person then spoke to Maya, who stood with folded hands in reverence.”

🔹 Verse 8
शृणुष्वैकमना: पूर्वं यदुक्तं ज्ञानमुत्तमम् ।
युगे युगे महर्षीणां स्वयमेव विवस्वता ॥

Word-by-word meaning:
शृणुष्व → listen
एकमना: → with one-pointed mind
पूर्वम् → earlier
यत् उक्तम् → that which was said
ज्ञानम् उत्तमम् → supreme knowledge
युगे युगे → in every age
महर्षीणाम् → to great sages
स्वयम् एव → by himself
विवस्वता → by the Sun

Overall meaning:
“Listen with a focused mind to this supreme knowledge which was previously taught by the Sun himself to the great sages in every age.”

🔹 Verse 9
शास्त्रमाद्यं तदेवेदं यत्पूर्वं प्राह भास्कर: ।
युगानां परिवर्तेन कालभेदोऽत्र केवल: ॥

Word-by-word meaning:
शास्त्रम् आद्यम् → the original science
तत् एव इदम् → this is that very same
यत् पूर्वम् → which earlier
प्राह → was spoken
भास्कर: → by the Sun
युगानाम् परिवर्तेन → due to change of yugas
काल भेद: → difference in time
अत्र केवल: → only this difference

Overall meaning:
“This is the very same original śāstra that was spoken earlier by the Sun; the only difference here is the variation in time due to the changing of yugas.”

🔹 Verse 10
Sanskrit:

लोकानामन्तकृत् काल: कालोऽन्य: कलनात्मक: ।
स द्विधा स्थूलसूक्ष्मत्वान्मूर्तश्चामूर्त उच्यते ॥

Word-by-word meaning:
लोकानाम् → of beings / worlds
अन्तकृत् → that which brings an end
काल: → time
काल: अन्य: → another kind of time
कलनात्मक: → used for calculation
स: → that
द्विधा → twofold
स्थूल → gross
सूक्ष्मत्वात् → subtle
मूर्त: → measurable / tangible
च → and
अमूर्त: → intangible

Overall meaning:
“Time is of two kinds — one that brings about the end of beings, and another that is used for calculation; it is further understood as twofold, as gross (tangible) and subtle (intangible).”

Overall summary:
As I reflect on these five verses together, what begins to emerge is not just an explanation, but a very carefully structured transition in the text. The Sun, who appeared as the teacher, first establishes a limitation — not in knowledge, but in form — saying that his brilliance cannot be directly endured even for a moment for the purpose of instruction. This immediately reframes the idea of teaching here; it is not that knowledge is unavailable, but that it must be transmitted in a way that is receivable. And so, instead of diminishing the knowledge, the Sun manifests a portion of himself — a representative — through whom the teaching can flow. This itself carries a deeper meaning: knowledge remains intact, but the medium adapts. Then the teaching begins, and the very first instruction is not about planets or calculations, but about attention — “listen with one-pointedness.” And what is to be listened to is not something new, not something being created in this moment, but something that has already been taught before, again and again, in every yuga, to great sages by the Sun himself. This removes any notion of novelty and places Jyotiṣa in the category of eternal knowledge — something that is revealed cyclically rather than invented. The next verse reinforces this even more strongly by saying that what is being taught now is exactly the same original śāstra, with the only difference being the variation of time across yugas. And that naturally leads into the final verse of this set, where the text finally begins to define time itself. Time is not treated as a single uniform concept; instead, it is divided into that which governs the life cycle of beings — bringing about creation and destruction — and that which is used for calculation and measurement. Further, even this measurable time is understood as having a gross and a subtle aspect, something that can be directly observed and something that is more underlying and intangible. In this way, the text quietly shifts from the narrative of transmission to the foundation of the subject itself, preparing the ground for a much deeper exploration of time, which is the very backbone of Jyotiṣa.
