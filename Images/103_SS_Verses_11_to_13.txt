ॐ गुरुर् ब्रह्मा गुरुर् विष्णुः
गुरुर् देवो महेश्वरः ।
गुरुः साक्षात् परं ब्रह्म
तस्मै श्री गुरवे नमः ॥

गुरवे सर्वलोकानां
भिषजे भव रोगिणाम् ।
निधये सर्वविद्यनाम्
दक्षिणामूर्तये नमः ॥

अस्मिन् गुरु चरणारविन्दाभ्यां नमः ॥

Today (04/24), we enter a very special section of the Sūrya Siddhānta.

Up to this point, the text has been building something very special. It began with a salutation to Brahman, the inconceivable foundation of all existence, reminding us that this knowledge does not begin with ordinary human effort alone. Then it introduced Maya’s intense tapas, born from a deep desire for the highest knowledge of Jyotiṣa, and showed us that the Sun himself responded with grace, granting that knowledge out of compassion and clarity. The teaching was then passed on in a proper line of transmission, not as a private possession, but as a living wisdom retaught across ages. And finally, the text made one of its most important early distinctions: time itself is not one simple thing, but has a dual character — one aspect that moves toward destruction and another that serves calculation. So now, as we enter verse 11, the text is ready to do something remarkable: it begins to give structure to that calculative time, starting not from the heavens, but from the most immediate rhythm of life itself.

Now, beginning with verse 11, the text takes a decisive step.

It begins to build time.

Not philosophically, but structurally. Measurably. Mathematically.

And what is astonishing is where it begins.

Not with the stars.

Not with the Sun.

But with your breath.

Let us listen to the verse.

Verse 11:

प्राणादिः कथितो मूर्तस्तुट्याद्योऽमूर्तसञ्चकः ।
षड्भिः प्राणैर्विनाडी स्यात्तत्षष्ट्या नाडिका स्मृता ॥ ११ ॥

Now let’s enter this slowly.

The verse begins with prāṇa, the breath. Not as a metaphor, but as a unit of time.

Take a moment and feel it.

Inhale…
Exhale…

That cycle, that lived physical duration, is one prāṇa.

The text calls this mūrta, measurable and tangible.

Alongside it, it also speaks of truṭi, an extremely subtle unit, called amūrta, not directly perceptible.

So right away, we are given two parallel worlds.

One you can feel.
One you can only infer.

Now comes the construction.

Take six such breaths.

Six prāṇas grouped together.

That becomes one vināḍī.

Now take that vināḍī and repeat it sixty times.

Sixty vināḍīs.

That becomes one nāḍī.

Now pause here and let’s connect this to modern understanding.

In contemporary physiology, a healthy adult at rest typically breathes about 12 to 16 times per minute.

If you extend that across a full day, that comes to roughly 17,000 to 23,000 breaths per day.

(pause)

Now think about what the text has done.

It has taken something we now measure scientifically and used it, thousands of years ago, as the very foundation of timekeeping.

Not an abstract second.

Not an arbitrary unit.

But something built directly from life itself.

(pause)

This is not exact in a modern laboratory sense, but it is remarkably close in spirit:

Verse 12:

नाडीषष्ट्या तु नाक्षत्रमहोरात्रं प्रकीर्तितम् ।
तत् त्रिंशता भवेन्मासः सावनोऽर्कोदयैस्तथा ॥ १२ ॥

Now the scale expands.

Take sixty nāḍīs.

That gives you one full day and night, ahorātra.

But notice something important.

This is not just any day.

It is tied to observation, sunrise.

This is called a sāvana day, a day measured from one sunrise to the next.

And here, it is worth being very precise.

The most scientifically careful way to mark sunrise or sunset is to use a fixed observing point and define it by the Sun’s center crossing the local horizon, with astronomical calculation for that exact location, date, and horizon conditions. In practice, that means sunrise is not just when it looks bright, but the moment the Sun’s center reaches the horizon line, adjusted for refraction and local terrain.

(pause)

So in the Sāvana framework, what matters is not vague daylight, but a repeatable daily reference point.

One sunrise to the next.
One sunrise-defined day.

(pause)

Now extend that.

Thirty such days.

Form one month.

So now we have a chain:

Breath → vināḍī → nāḍī → day → month

Each step cleanly built from the previous.

No gaps. No ambiguity.

But the text is not done refining.

Now comes a crucial distinction.

Verse 13:

म्ऐन्दवस्तिथिभस्तद्वत् संक्रान्त्या सौर उच्यते ।
मासैर्द्वादशभिर्वर्षं दिव्यं तदह उच्यते ॥ १३ ॥

This verse is doing something very elegant.

It compares two systems of time reckoning.

It says that just as the lunar month is determined by tithis, the solar reckoning is determined by saṅkrānti.

That word saṅkrānti is important.

It means a solar ingress, the Sun’s movement from one sign into the next.

So the text is saying:

The lunar month is defined by lunar phase divisions.
The solar month is defined by the Sun’s passage through the zodiac.

(pause)

That is the real distinction here.

One system listens to the Moon’s changing light.
The other follows the Sun’s motion through the sky.

(pause)

And then the verse says:

Twelve months make one divine year.

That is a complete solar year, expressed as a higher or more refined measure.

So the point is not just “a year is twelve months.”

The point is that the year is being defined in a solar-divine framework, with the Sun’s motion as the governing reference.

(pause)

Now let’s step back for a moment.

What have these three verses done?

They started with your breath.

Something intimate. Immediate.

They scaled it, carefully and systematically, into days and months.

And then they introduced a more refined distinction:

The Moon gives us tithis and lunar months.

The Sun gives us saṅkrānti and solar months.

And the year, in this framework, is twelve such months.

(pause)

This is the birth of astronomical time.

Not just counting time.

But tuning it to reality.

Lets do a quick roll up: 
In summary, these verses build time from breath upward. They show how prāṇa becomes vināḍī, vināḍī becomes nāḍī, and nāḍī becomes day and month. They also distinguish lunar reckoning from solar reckoning, with saṅkrānti marking the solar measure. In this way, the text moves from bodily rhythm to celestial order.
