Sūrya Siddhānta is one of the foundational texts in the Indian astronomical tradition. It presents a systematic approach to understanding planetary motion, time cycles, and celestial order.

What is particularly notable is the way the text begins.

It does not begin directly with calculation.
It begins with invocation, inquiry, and preparation.

In this recording, I will go through the first five verses of the text.

I will first recite these five verses.

अचिन्त्याव्यक्तरूपाय निर्गुणाय गुणात्मने ।
समस्तजगदाधार-मूर्तये ब्रह्मणे नमः ॥ १ ॥

अल्पावशिष्टे तु कृते मयो नाम महासुरः ।

वेदाङ्गमग्र्यमखिलं ज्योतिषां गतिकारणम् ।
आराधयन् विवस्वन्तं तपस्तेपे सुदुश्चरम् ॥ ३ ॥

तोषितस्तपसा तेन प्रीतस्तस्मै वरार्थिने ।
ग्रहाणां चरितं प्रादान्मयाय सविता स्वयम् ॥ ४ ॥

श्रीसूर्य उवाच ।
विदितस्ते मया भावस्तोषितस्तपसा ह्यहम् ।

First Verse

अचिन्त्याव्यक्तरूपाय निर्गुणाय गुणात्मने ।
समस्तजगदाधार-मूर्तये ब्रह्मणे नमः ॥ १ ॥

The text begins with a salutation to Brahman.

The words used here describe that reality as:

beyond thought — अचिन्त्य
unmanifest — अव्यक्तरूप
beyond attributes — निर्गुण
and yet the basis of all attributes — गुणात्मन्

It then says that this is the support of the entire universe —
समस्त-जगत्-आधार.

So before entering into the study of celestial motion, the text establishes a foundational perspective.

All observable movement is understood as resting upon an underlying, unmanifest reality. This was the first verse.

Now, lets get into Second Verse

अल्पावशिष्टे तु कृते मयो नाम महासुरः ।
रहस्यं परमं पुण्यं जिज्ञासुर्ज्ञानमुत्तमम् ॥ २ ॥

Now the text introduces a seeker — Maya, described as a great being.

The important word here is:

जिज्ञासुः — one who desires to know.

He seeks a parama rahasyam — a profound and sacred knowledge.

This indicates that the subject matter is not presented casually. It is something to be sought with intent and attention.

So the progression moves from invocation to inquiry.

Third Verse

वेदाङ्गमग्र्यमखिलं ज्योतिषां गतिकारणम् ।
आराधयन् विवस्वन्तं तपस्तेपे सुदुश्चरम् ॥ ३ ॥

Here we understand what is being sought.

The subject is Jyotiṣa, described as:

a principal Vedāṅga
that which explains the causes of celestial movement — गतिकारणम्

In order to obtain this knowledge, Maya performs tapas, worshipping Vivasvān, the Sun.

This is significant.

The Sun is central to the measurement of time and the observation of motion. Approaching the Sun for this knowledge is therefore consistent within this framework.

At the same time, the emphasis is on effort and discipline — knowledge is approached through tapas.

Fourth Verse

तोषितस्तपसा तेन प्रीतस्तस्मै वरार्थिने ।
ग्रहाणां चरितं प्रादान्मयाय सविता स्वयम् ॥ ४ ॥

In this verse, the result of that effort is described.

The Sun, being pleased, grants Maya the knowledge of the grahas — their movement and functioning.

The word used is चरितम्, indicating their behavior or course.

So the text now transitions from seeking to receiving.

Fifth Verse

श्रीसूर्य उवाच ।
विदितस्ते मया भावस्तोषितस्तपसा ह्यहम् ।
दद्यां कालाश्रयं ज्ञानं ग्रहाणां चरितं महत् ॥ ५ ॥

Now Sūrya speaks.

He says:

“I understand your intention. I am pleased by your tapas. I will give you great knowledge of the grahas.”

The key phrase here is:

कालाश्रयं ज्ञानम् — knowledge based on time.

This is fundamental to the entire text.

All astronomical understanding is structured around time:

positions are defined with respect to time
motion is defined with respect to time
cycles are defined with respect to time

So what is being given is not isolated information, but a structured framework based on time.

Summary

If we look at these five verses together, there is a clear sequence:

Invocation of Brahman
Emergence of inquiry
Seeking of higher knowledge
Effort through tapas
Granting of knowledge
Beginning of structured teaching

This forms the proper introduction to Sūrya Siddhānta. I will stop this recording here.
